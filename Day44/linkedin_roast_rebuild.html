LinkedIn search and recruiter search filters run heavily on headline keywords. "Open to Work" contains zero searchable skills — no "Data Engineer," no "Python," no "SQL." You are invisible in search results for roles you actually want.

"Fresher with a one year of experience"
Recruiter's 3-second reaction:

This is the line that shows before "see more." A recruiter reads it, mentally files you as junior-and-unsure, and taps away. "Fresher" and "one year of experience" are contradictory framings used together — it reads like you're not sure how to position yourself, so the recruiter decides for you (badly). Nothing here says what you actually do or who you help.

🔍 The invisible cost

Most recruiters spend under 10 seconds before deciding to click "see more" or not. This line loses that click almost every time — meaning the rest of your About section, however good, is never read.

There isn't a full section to score beyond the one line you gave me — which is itself the finding. A one-line About section on a job-search profile tells a recruiter you either haven't invested in your narrative or don't know what your narrative is.

Experience 3/10

Data Engineer, Prime Techwings — "Works with Dataflows, Daily Scrums, Conduct Meetings, Manage data pipelines"
Recruiter's 3-second reaction:

"This reads like a job description someone else wrote, not a person describing their own work." Four fragments, no verbs in past/present-continuous form, no scale, no outcome. "Conduct Meetings" and "Daily Scrums" tell me you attend ceremonies — they don't tell me you built anything.

🧠 Why it hurts

Recruiters read experience bullets looking for evidence of impact: pipelines that moved X volume of data, dashboards that saved Y hours, processes you improved. "Manage data pipelines" with no numbers or tools named could describe literally anyone.

🔍 The invisible cost

You're a Data Engineer with real tooling experience, and none of it is named here. Recruiters searching for specific stack keywords (Spark, Airflow, cloud platforms) have no way to find you through this entry.

DAY 4

Comment on 5 Posts in Your Target Space

The Value Comment formula: Agree with a specific point → Add a genuine insight or your own experience → Ask a question that invites a reply. Example: "Completely agree that pipeline monitoring gets overlooked early on. In my own work I've found [specific observation]. Curious — how do you decide when it's worth automating alerts vs. manual checks?" This gets you seen by the poster and their audience, not just the original poster.

DAY 5

Publish Post #2 — Lessons-Learned / Hot Take

Unpopular opinion: "Manage data pipelines" is the most useless line on most Data Engineer resumes and profiles — mine included, until this week. It tells a recruiter nothing. Managed how? At what scale? Solving what problem? The fix wasn't complicated — I just had to actually say what I did: which systems, what broke, what I fixed, what got better because of it. If your experience section reads like a job description instead of a story, that's the first thing I'd change. What's a phrase you've retired from your own profile?
~640 characters.

DAY 7

Review & Adjust

Profile views: A jump this week vs. your prior baseline means your headline/keywords are now surfacing in search — good sign. Flat views mean the keyword set may need to be more specific to your target roles.

Connection acceptance rate: Above ~40-50% on cold requests is healthy for this template. Lower means either your targeting is too broad or your profile (photo, headline) isn't building enough trust at a glance — revisit Day 1 changes.

Post engagement: Comments matter more than likes here — they're what extends reach. If Day 2's post underperformed, Day 5's question should be sharper and more specific to get replies, not just agreement.
