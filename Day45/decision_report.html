The Real Decision

With 2–3 months to land a Data Engineer or AI Engineer role, you have to split limited time across three levers: deepening your AI Data Engineering Mentor project, earning DP-700 / AWS AI Practitioner certifications, or applying to jobs now with smaller portfolio pieces.

The real trade-off is depth vs. breadth vs. speed — a standout project builds proof, certifications build credibility signals, and applying now builds momentum and feedback.

It's emotionally hard because all three feel necessary, and your fear isn't any one path failing — it's picking wrong and losing weeks you don't have.

The Case For Each Option

Option A — Deepen the AI Data Engineering Mentor Project

Strongest caseA polished, deployed project is concrete proof you can build real AI + data engineering systems — something interviewers can click through, not just hear about.
Hidden upsideIt becomes reusable interview material — you can walk through architecture decisions in behavioral and technical rounds instead of reciting theory.
Biggest weaknessDeep work on one project can quietly consume your whole runway with nothing to show recruiters until it's "done."
Best if you value: demonstrable, practical proof over credentials

Option B — Certifications (DP-700, AWS AI Practitioner)

Strongest caseCertifications are fast, recognizable credibility signals that can get you past ATS filters and recruiter skims where a project link won't be opened.
Hidden upsideStudying for them also sharpens the vocabulary and concepts you'll need to sound fluent in interviews, even beyond the badge itself.
Biggest weaknessA certification alone doesn't prove you can build anything — without a project to back it up, it's a credential with no story behind it.
Best if you value: fast credibility and clearing automated screens

Option C — Apply Now + Smaller Portfolio Projects

Strongest caseApplying earlier starts the interview pipeline sooner — and given your 2–3 month goal, real feedback from real interviews is the fastest way to learn what's actually blocking you.
Hidden upsideSmaller, varied projects can be shipped fast, giving you more distinct portfolio pieces to talk about instead of betting everything on one.
Biggest weaknessSpreading across many small projects risks a portfolio that looks scattered instead of showing deep, standout expertise.
Best if you value: speed, momentum, and real-world feedback loops

Premortem: 12 Months From Now, It Failed

Option A — Deepen the Project

3 reasons it failed
  • You kept polishing features instead of shipping and applying, so no recruiter ever saw it.
  • The project stayed impressive to you but wasn't framed for interviews, so it didn't translate into callbacks.
  • You skipped certifications entirely and got filtered out by ATS keyword screens before a human saw the project.
Early warning sign

3–4 weeks in, you're still adding features and haven't sent a single application or resume update.

Prevention action

Set a hard "ship and apply" date now — not a "feature complete" date.

Option C — Apply Now + Smaller Projects

3 reasons it failed
  • Applications went out but interviews stalled because the portfolio felt thin next to other candidates' deeper projects.
  • Smaller projects were too generic to differentiate you from other Data Engineering applicants.
  • Without certifications, you got filtered out early by keyword-based ATS systems.
Early warning sign

After 2 weeks of applications, you're getting few or no responses and no interview requests.

Prevention action

Track response rate weekly — if it's near zero after 10–15 applications, pause and strengthen the one strongest project before continuing to apply.

The Verdict

Option A: Deepen the Project — in parallel with certification study

It wins because it scores highest on growth, long-term alignment, and overall total, and it's the option your own gut already picked — the fear stopping you was about neglecting the others, not about the project itself.

What could flip it: if Day 7 of your test plan shows zero interview traction from the project, or if early applications reveal ATS keyword filtering is the real blocker — in that case, shift weight toward certifications and applications immediately.

The hard truth: you don't have a prioritization problem, you have a sequencing problem — you're deciding what to do first, not what to do only, and stalling on that choice is its own cost.
